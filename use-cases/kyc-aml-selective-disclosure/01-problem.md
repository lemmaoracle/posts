# Problem

KYC (Know Your Customer) and AML (Anti-Money Laundering) are core regulatory compliance functions for financial institutions. Yet their practice rests on a structural dilemma: **proving compliance requires sharing data, and that sharing itself generates privacy and regulatory risk.**

The current framework exhibits the following structural defects:

- **Duplicate submissions:** Customers repeatedly submit the same identity documents, income certificates, shareholder structures, and transaction histories to each financial institution.
- **Cross-border PII transfers:** Information sharing with overseas entities or remittance destinations causes PII to cross borders, requiring GDPR, APPI, and data protection compliance assessments each time.
- **Centralized KYC utility breach risk:** Shared databases appear efficient but expand the damage scope organization-wide when breached.
- **Audit trail fragmentation:** Each institution independently claims "we completed KYC," but there is no cryptographic means for third parties to verify consistency.

Regulatory pressure is intensifying. The FATF Travel Rule (mandatory identity information sharing between crypto-asset service providers), GDPR extraterritorial application, revised APPI, and FSA AI governance guidelines — all simultaneously demand "data sharing for compliance" and "data minimization principles."

The premise that "you cannot prove without sharing" must be rewritten by technology.
